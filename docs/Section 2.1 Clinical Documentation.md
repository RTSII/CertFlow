**Section 2.1 – Clinical Documentation** focuses on how clinical information is created, entered, stored, and corrected in the EHR. As a future CEHRS, you’re the link between:

## Part 1: Big Picture – What Is Clinical Documentation?

**Clinical documentation** is the **official, permanent record** of:

- Patient encounters  
- Clinical observations  
- Assessments, diagnoses  
- Orders and treatments  
- Responses and outcomes

It’s used for:

- **Continuity of care** – so every provider knows what happened before.  
- **Billing and coding** – “If it isn’t documented, it didn’t happen” (from a billing perspective).  
- **Legal evidence** – the record is a legal document.  
- **Quality measurement & reporting** – for regulatory programs and internal improvement.

## Part 2: Core Concepts You Must Know

### 6.1 Data Types in the EHR

In CEHRS, you need to think in terms of **data types**, not just “notes.”

#### 1. Demographic / Administrative Data

Examples:

- Name, DOB, sex, address, phone  
- Insurance, guarantor information  
- Emergency contacts  
- Primary care provider

**Why it matters:** Mis-typed demographics can lead to **duplicate charts**, **misidentification**, or **denied claims**.

#### 2. Clinical Data

Examples:

- Chief complaint, HPI (history of present illness)  
- Past medical/surgical history  
- Allergies, medications  
- Vital signs, labs, imaging results  
- Assessments, diagnoses, plans  
- Orders and procedures

#### 3. Financial / Operational Data

Examples:

- Charge capture entries  
- Coding and modifiers  
- Billing status, claim numbers  
- Encounter type, location, provider

All three categories **interact** in the EHR; errors in one can affect the others.

---

### 6.2 Source vs. Derived Data

**Source data** = Original entries made at the time of care.

- Example: Nurse documents “BP 160/95 at 10:05 am.”  
- Example: Provider writes an assessment “Uncontrolled hypertension.”

**Derived data** = Data that’s **calculated, summarized, or generated** from source data.

- Example: EHR generates a trend graph of BP readings.  
- Example: Clinical decision support triggers a “High BP” alert.  
- Example: Quality report counts how many hypertensive patients have BP controlled.

> **CEHRS angle:** You must respect and protect **source data integrity**, and understand how **derived data** is used in reporting and quality programs.

---

### 6.3 Documentation Standards & Best Practices

Think of good documentation as having 5 core qualities:

1. **Accurate**
   - Reflects what truly happened; no guessing or “filling in later.”
2. **Complete**
   - Includes required elements (e.g., date, time, provider, signature) and clinically relevant details.
3. **Timely**
   - Entered as close to the time of service as possible.
4. **Legible & Organized**
   - In EHRs, this means using correct fields, avoiding random free-text where structured fields exist.
5. **Objective & Professional**
   - No blaming language or personal opinions; stick to observable facts and clinical reasoning.

#### Try This

In your notes, write **one example of poor documentation** you might see in real life, then rewrite it as **better documentation**.

Example:

- Poor: “Pt non-compliant and rude.”  
- Better: “Patient reports not taking antihypertensive medications for the past 3 weeks due to cost. Patient raised voice during discussion and stated, ‘I don’t see why I need these pills.’”

### 6.4 Common Clinical Documents & Sections

You don’t need to memorize every type of note, but you should recognize the major ones.

#### 1. History & Physical (H&P)

Usually done at **admission or first visit**.

- Chief complaint  
- History of present illness  
- Past medical/surgical history  
- Family and social history  
- Review of systems  
- Physical exam  
- Assessment and plan

#### 2. Progress Notes

Document ongoing care (daily or per visit).

- Focus on **changes**, **responses to treatment**, and **new issues**.

Common structures: **SOAP** (Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan) or **DAP** (Data, Assessment, Plan).

#### 3. Orders and Results

- Medications, labs, imaging, referrals.  
- Results returned and must be **reviewed and acknowledged**.

CEHRS relevance: Tracking that **results are acted on** is a safety and documentation issue.

#### 4. Problem List and Medication List

- **Problem list** = active and resolved diagnoses/conditions.  
- **Medication list** = current and sometimes historical meds.

You may help ensure these lists are **current, non-duplicated, and accurate.**

#### 5. Flowsheets

- Used for **repetitive data**: vitals, I&O, ventilator settings, etc.  
- Often structured rows and columns in EHR.

---

### 6.5 Amendments, Corrections, and Late Entries

You **will** see questions on this in CEHRS.

Key principles:

1. **Never delete or alter original entries.**
   - The EHR must maintain an **audit trail**.
2. **Corrections / Amendments**
   - Add a corrected entry that:
     - Clearly indicates it is a correction.
     - References the original entry (date/time, what is being corrected).
     - Includes your name, credentials, date, and time.
3. **Late Entries**
   - If you document after the fact, label it as a **late entry** and include:
     - Current date/time of the entry.
     - The date/time of the event being documented.
     - Reason or context, if appropriate.

Example wording:

> “Late entry for 09/10/2025 at 0900: Patient received influenza vaccine in left deltoid, tolerated well. No adverse reaction noted as of 0930. – J. Smith, RN, 09/10/2025 1100.”

### 6.6 Legal, Ethical, and Regulatory Considerations

Clinical documentation must comply with:

- **HIPAA** – privacy and security of PHI.  
- **HITECH / meaningful use / interoperability programs** – structured data for reporting and exchange.  
- **State laws** – retention periods, access rules, corrections procedures.

Ethical principles:

- **Honesty** – never falsify documentation.  
- **Confidentiality** – only access and document on patients you are authorized to.  
- **Professionalism** – avoid judgmental, biased, or casual language.

- Circle any items that would need **updating or correction** at the next visit.

## Part 6: CEHRS Exam Tips for Clinical Documentation

When you see **documentation questions** on the CEHRS exam:

- Look for answers that:
  - Preserve **integrity of the record** (no deletion/overwriting).  
  - Emphasize **timely, accurate, objective** entries.  
  - Respect **legal and ethical standards** (HIPAA, audit trail, no falsification).
- Be cautious of options that:
  - Suggest changing or hiding errors without an audit trail.  
  - Use judgmental or emotional language in the chart.  
  - Encourage documenting **before** something happens (e.g., charting meds before giving them).

If you’re stuck, ask yourself:

> “Which option would a compliance officer, risk manager, or HIM director approve of?”

That’s usually your best bet.

## Key Terms Glossary

- **Clinical Documentation** – The official record of patient encounters, including history, findings, assessments, and plans.
- **Source Data** – Original information recorded at the time of care (e.g., vital signs entry).
- **Derived Data** – Information generated from source data (e.g., calculated BMI, trend graphs, quality metrics).
- **Problem List** – Ongoing list of a patient’s diagnoses and conditions.
- **Medication List** – Current (and sometimes historical) medications a patient is taking.
- **Late Entry** – Documentation of an event after the time it occurred, clearly labeled with both event and entry times.
- **Addendum / Amendment** – A supplementary entry that adds or clarifies information in an existing note without altering the original.
- **Audit Trail** – A log that records who accessed or changed information in the EHR and when.
- **Copy-Paste (Copy-Forward)** – Reusing prior documentation text; can save time but must be carefully edited to reflect the current encounter.
- **Objective Language** – Factual, observable, non-judgmental statements in the record.
